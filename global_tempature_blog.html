The hole we dug ourselves into.

Hi climate-interested folks,
I recently came by an article, that was published by unenvironment.org and thought about sharing and summarising what they had to say as well as explain the consequences.
The article was about the rising global temperature and how greenhouse gas emissions destroy our planet and shared some interesting data such as that 2019 and 2016 were the hottest recorded years to date. All of this is unarguably the direct cause of the heat-trapping greenhouse gases. Once we hit that very temperature mark though, things will probably go downhill to the point of irreversible change.
Personally, (and as you’d learn about in the addition to the other blogs) this information goes to show and makes clear in how badly of a situation we currently brought ourselves into. Matters only get worse the longer this trend continues as global temperatures affect everyone and everything as we and our environment literally melt before our eyes.

The consequences are shocking to think of.

I also found two videos from news.sky.com that give a scary outlook on how the earth is going to look if we don't act accordingly. One presents the scenario of reaching 2 degrees Celsius (that isn't even that unrealistic). The other one portrays the apocalyptic scenario of reaching 4-5 degrees Celsius average Temperature if worldwide cooperation and measures fail. The 2-degree mark is the Target to lowering the increase of temperature caused by humans but reaching 2 degrees might have big consequences for our whole planet's environment too. If the temperature rises by only one-degree ⅓ of our planet's freshwater might be gone in only 85 years due to ice and glaciers melting away in the mountains. The vanishing of the ice on the poles leads to a self-reinforcing global warming because there isn't enough ice to reflect heat, which warms up the globe even further. A development we can already observe now is that nations that suffer from natural disasters like hurricanes and tsunamis will get hit even harder the more the globe warms up. The rising sea levels due to ice melting could by 2100 deplace 10 percent of the world's population while ⅓ of all live on earth faces extinction.
You remember the self-reinforcing effect i talked about earlier? If we don't manage to work together to stop global warming and just continue emitting greenhouse gases in huge amounts, we might end up with the apocalyptic version of the known consequences of climate change. If the Global average Temperature rises to 4-5 degrees the planet will soon not be habitable anymore with all species being vanished and the last humans fighting for survival on the rapidly decreasing cultivable soil.
We need to work together internationally to possibly prevent all of these consequences and too keep our planet a habitable place.

Turtles threatened with extinction!

Recently I read an article: " Why global temperatures matter" According to this article the sweet sea turtles are actually threatened with extention! Global Warming is actually a huge problem. It affects the whole world. Since the temperature of the beach sand that female sea turtles nest in influences the gender of their offspring during the breeding season, our warming climate may be driving sea turtles into extinction by creating a shortage of males, according to several studies. A few degrees make a huge difference. At sand temperatures of 31.1 degrees Celsius, only female green sea turtles hatch, while at 27.8 degrees Celsius and below, only males hatch. If the balance is upset the future of the turtles will be in danger! Many species on this planet have a similar situation. It’s a fact that all natural and human systems are sensitive to climate warming in varying degrees. In order to estimate the likely impact of global warming on our planet at various temperature limits above pre-industrial levels the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) published a Special Report on Global Warming of 1.5 degrees Celsius in October 2019. The IPCC is the United Nations body responsible for assessing the science related to climate change. They examined the impacts of limiting the increase in global average temperature to well below 2 degrees Celsius above pre-industrial levels. They also projected the effects that the earth is likely to experience both a warming of 1.5 degrees Celsius and a warming of 2 degrees Celsius above those levels. The 1.5-degree Celsius threshold represents the target goal established by the Paris Agreement, adopted by 195 nations in december 2015 to address the threat of climate change. The time is running out! Now it´s time to act and to save the turtles. Now it´s time to save our planet!

Comparison of damage inbetween 1.5 and 2° C global warming.

Now after looking into the many reasons why Earth’s natural and human systems are sensitive to climate change, it would also be worth looking into the specific ways in which our planet change in the future, considering global warming temperatures increase another half, full degree Celsius - or possibly even beyond.
A key point of the whole problem is that while the whole world warms up, it’s worth noting that not all regions suffer in the same ways or as much as others.
In most cases, regions under a hotter temperature threshold than average were found to experience higher climate-related risks for their natural and human systems than others, mainly due to different availability of means to mitigate and adapt to climate change in the region. Climate change in the world never was spread evenly, and never will be in future either. For example, some regions as small island states will be subject to experience multiple climate-related issues that will compound on top of each other.
In many regions, warming has already surpassed 1.5° C in comparison to pre-industrial levels - more than a fifth of the worlds population has experienced warming greater than 1.5° C for at least one season. With further increase of global warming effects, most land regions will see more hot days - especially already disadvantaged people and communities. At 1.5° C of global warming, about 14 percent of the total world population will face severe heatwaves, at least once every half a decade - at just 0.5° C more, that number already increases towards 37 percent.
Limiting warming to 1.5° C would reduce the number of people frequently facing extreme heatwaves by about 420 million,
with 65 million fewer people exposed to exceptional heatwaves. And while it might be a harsh reality to face - 1.5° C of global warming would already mean that earths mid-latitudes, for instance, will already face their hottest days being 3° C warmer; the problem is worst for a lot of regions actually causing the least in terms of the underlying problem.
The regions facing warmest extreme temperatures will be in Central and Eastern North America, Central and South Europe, the Mediterranean with Northern Africa and the near-East, Western and Central Asia and Southern Africa.
At 2 degrees Celsius total warming, deadly heatwaves as those in India and Pakistan in 2015 may become an annual occurence.
The coldest regions - earths high-latitudes will see warming equivalent to a triple or even a quadruple of the total global warming - that is, with 1.5° C global warming, regions as the Arctic will see coldest events being warmer by up to a whole 4.5-6 degrees Celsius.
Let’s compare a warming of 1.5° with 2° in the case of a few specific key problems:
Droughts:
Limiting warming to 1.5° C will significantly reduce drought probability and risks related to water inavailability in some regions - particularly those in the Mediterranean, Southern Africa, South America and Australia.
61 million additional humans will be exposed to drought-related issues if we let global warming rise to a total 2° C.
Water Availability:
Around 50% less people will be affected by climate change-induced water stress by limiting global warming to 1.5° C. Herein, the most severely vulnerable people are those living in river basins, especially those in the Middle and Near East. If we limit warming to 1.5° C, that means between 190 and 270 million fewer people will be exposed to water scarcity by 2050 than with 2° C of total warming.
Extreme Precipitation (flooding):
Some places will see an increase in heavy rainfall events at 2° C compared to at 1.5° C. That is, especially the Northern Hemisphere regions - namely Alaska, Western and Eastern Canada, Greenland, Iceland, Northern Europe and Northern Asia will see an increase in heavy rainfall and flooding risks, with more of Earths land areas being vulnerable to the effects of increased runoff, aswell as other regions seeing a similar increase of a less significant level - both in frequency and intensity of heavy precipitation events.
Impacts on Biodiversity and Ecosystems:
A lot of animals see their climatically determined geographic range reduce due to the effects of climate change. At 1.5° C warming, 6% of insects, 8% of plants and 4% of vertebrates will see that range decrease by a whole half - with 2°C warming, those numbers jumping to 18%, 16% and 8%, respectively. It’s worth noting that due to the decreasing range of animals, there’s a significant effect projecting onto vegetation, for instance, with pollinating insects such as bees supporting terrestrial productivity being absent.
Aside of that - obviously - heat-related events such as Fires may become more likely at 2° C compared to 1.5° C. Adding on top of that, warming increase also supports explosive spread of invasive species that may bring the whole ecosystem of a region out of its natural order. It will also lead to reduction of rainforest biomass and increase wildfires - trees at the southern boundaries of boreal forests will die.
Ocean Impacts:
1.5° C of total warming will already see sea levels rise steadily, as science finds the heat stored in the oceans from human-produced warming will keep on causing them to expand. But the increase of sea levels is projected to be 0.1 meters lower at 1.5 degrees Celsius total warming than at 2 degrees.
About 10 million fewer people would be exposed to the risks bound to sea level increase by 2100 at the 1.5° C threshold, assuming humans don’t adapt.
Slowing the rate of sea level rise will see ecological systems have the space to better adapt, particularly in small islands and low-altitude regions.
Impacts on humans:
With 1.5° C of total warming, we will see health-related risks for humans increase - and at 2° C, the severity will only increase. Disadvantaged populations with livelihoods based on agriculture or coastal resource will be at highest risk.
Limiting the warming to 1.5° C will see us the number of people susceptible to climate-related poverty risks decreased by several hundred million by 2050, in comparison to 2° C.
Heat-Related illnesses also become more likely with increasing warming - scientists find that especially those with chronic illnesses are the most susceptible to those risks.
